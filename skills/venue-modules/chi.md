# CHI Venue Module

**ACM Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems**

CHI is the premier venue for human-computer interaction research. It values rigorous user studies, design contributions, and understanding of human behavior with technology. Unlike UIST, CHI does not require a working system — empirical studies, design frameworks, and theoretical contributions are welcome.

---

## Core Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Significance of the Contribution
- Does this matter to the HCI community?
- Does it address a real problem that affects real people?
- Is the scale of impact proportional to the claims?

### 2. Originality
- Is the research question, method, or framing genuinely new?
- Does it go beyond applying known methods to a new domain?
- Is there an intellectual surprise?

### 3. Rigor
- Is the methodology appropriate for the claims?
- Are the study design, analysis, and interpretation sound?
- Are limitations acknowledged and discussed?

### 4. Contribution Type
CHI explicitly categorizes contributions:
- **Empirical:** New findings about human behavior, needs, or practices
- **Artifact:** Novel system, tool, or design
- **Methodological:** New research method or framework
- **Theoretical:** New theory, model, or conceptual framework
- **Survey/Opinion:** Comprehensive review or position paper

Your paper should clearly identify its contribution type and be evaluated accordingly.

---

## What CHI Reviewers Look For

### Strong Accept Signals
- Deep qualitative insight that changes how we think about a practice
- A rigorous mixed-methods study that triangulates findings
- A design framework grounded in extensive empirical work
- A system contribution with both novel interaction AND thorough evaluation
- A study that reveals something surprising about human behavior with technology

### Common Acceptance Patterns
- **Formative Study → Design Implications:** Interview/observe users → derive actionable insights for designers
- **System + Mixed-Methods Evaluation:** Build a system → evaluate with both quantitative and qualitative methods
- **Large-Scale Survey + Interviews:** Breadth (survey N=500+) → Depth (interviews N=15-25)
- **Diary Study / Longitudinal:** Track behavior over time → identify patterns and shifts
- **Design Workshops / Participatory Design:** Involve stakeholders → derive design requirements

### Red Flags
- "We built a system and users liked it" with no deeper analysis
- Small N with quantitative-only evaluation
- Qualitative coding with no inter-rater reliability
- Claims of generalizability from non-representative samples
- Technology-driven work disguised as HCI (AI wrapper with thin user study)

## Study Design Patterns

### Qualitative Methods
| Method | When to Use | Typical N |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Semi-structured interviews | Understanding practices, needs, experiences | 12-25 |
| Contextual inquiry | Understanding work practices in situ | 8-15 |
| Diary study | Tracking behavior over time | 15-30 |
| Focus groups | Exploring group dynamics and shared understanding | 3-6 groups of 4-8 |
| Thematic analysis | Finding patterns across qualitative data | — |

### Quantitative Methods
| Method | When to Use | Typical N |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Controlled experiment | Comparing specific conditions | 24-48 (within-subjects) |
| Survey | Measuring attitudes, prevalence | 200-1000+ |
| Log analysis | Understanding behavior at scale | 1000+ |
| A/B test | Comparing two designs | 100+ per condition |

### Mixed Methods
- **Sequential explanatory:** Quantitative first → qualitative to explain patterns
- **Sequential exploratory:** Qualitative first → quantitative to validate/scale
- **Concurrent triangulation:** Both methods simultaneously, compare findings

## Paper Structure (Typical)

```
1. INTRODUCTION (~1.5 pages)
   - Motivating scenario or problem
   - Research questions (explicitly stated)
   - Contribution summary

2. RELATED WORK (~2 pages)
   - Theoretical framing
   - Empirical context
   - Design space positioning

3. METHODOLOGY (~2-3 pages)
   - Study design and rationale
   - Participants and recruitment
   - Data collection procedures
   - Analysis methods

4. FINDINGS (~4-5 pages)
   - Themes/results organized by research question
   - Rich quotes and examples for qualitative work
   - Tables and figures for quantitative work

5. DISCUSSION (~2-3 pages)
   - Interpretation of findings
   - Design implications
   - Limitations and future work
   - Broader impact

6. CONCLUSION (~0.5 page)
```

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Thin evaluation:** System paper with only a usability study, no deeper analysis
2. **Overclaimed generalizability:** Small, non-representative sample with broad claims
3. **Missing positionality:** No discussion of researcher's role and potential biases
4. **Methodology mismatch:** Using the wrong method for the research question
5. **No design implications:** Findings without actionable takeaways for the community
6. **Scope creep:** Trying to cover too many research questions in one paper
7. **Known result:** "Discovering" something the community already knows well

---

## Taste Calibration for CHI

When applying the 5 taste tests for CHI:
- **Fundamental vs Surface:** Does this reveal something structural about human-technology interaction, or just test a feature?
- **Elegance:** Can the core finding be expressed as a principle that designers can apply broadly?
- **Generative Power:** Will other researchers use this framework/finding/method in their own work?
- **Pairwise:** Compare against CHI Best Papers and Honorable Mentions
- **Senior Reviewer:** Would an AC who has seen hundreds of studies find this insightful or routine?
